Solutions & Resources

What Needs to Change

Based on this research, improving breast and cervical cancer outcomes requires more than medical advances it requires improving access, awareness, and trust in the healthcare system.

Key Recommendations

  • Increase access to routine care

    • Expand availability of primary care providers

    • Encourage annual checkups as a gateway to prevention

  • Reduce financial barriers

    • Expand low-cost and free screening programs

    • Improve insurance coverage and affordability

  • Strengthen continuity of care

    • Increase access to personal healthcare providers

    • Support long-term patient-provider relationships

  • Improve health literacy and awareness

    • Provide clear, culturally relevant education on screening

    • Increase awareness of HPV, Pap tests, and mammograms

    • Ensure patients understand when and why to get screened

  • Address structural inequities

    • Invest in underserved communities

    • Expand mobile clinics and community-based care

Atlanta-Based Resources

🏥 Free & Low-Cost Screening Services

1. Grady Health System (Atlanta)

  • Offers breast and cervical cancer screenings

  • Sliding scale fees and financial assistance available

  • Strong safety-net system serving uninsured populations
    👉 https://www.gradyhealth.org

2. Fulton County Board of Health

  • Breast and cervical cancer screening programs

  • Family planning + preventive services

  • Multiple clinic locations across Atlanta
    👉 https://fultoncountyboh.com

3. DeKalb County Board of Health

  • Pap tests, HPV services, and women’s health screenings

  • Low-cost services for eligible residents
    👉 https://dekalbpublichealth.com/

4. Georgia Breast and Cervical Cancer Program (BCCP)

  • FREE breast & cervical cancer screenings for eligible women

  • Covers mammograms, Pap tests, and diagnostic follow-up

  • Can also help with treatment coverage if diagnosed
    👉  https://dph.georgia.gov/BCCP

🚐 Mobile & Community-Based Care

5. Black Women’s Wellness (Atlanta-based)

  • Provides education, navigation, and screening support

6. Sisters by Choice

7. HEAL Clinic (Morehouse School of Medicine)

When Should You Get Screened? (Quick Guide)

  • Mammogram: Starting around age 40 (talk to your provider)

  • Pap test: Ages 21–65

  • HPV vaccine: Ages 11–12 (can be given later if missed)

Final Takeaway

Early detection saves lives….but only if people have:

  • Access to care

  • Affordable services

  • Clear information

  • Support to navigate the system

Prevention is possible. Equity is the missing piece.
